Reply from Kevin Lu
TOPIC OVERVIEW: The most surprising finding from my research was that the Pentagon blacklisted Anthropic for refusing to remove two safety restrictions (mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons), and then hours later signed a deal with OpenAI that included essentially the same restrictions. That sequence of events suggests the dispute was never really about the safeguards themselves but about establishing the principle that when the government buys your technology, you don't get to set conditions. I want a general audience to understand what happened, why it matters for civil liberties, and why we need federal legislation governing military AI so that these questions don't come down to contract negotiations between a handful of CEOs and whoever is running the Pentagon.

PROPOSED GENRE/PLATFORM: Op-ed, formatted for a general-interest tech publication like The Verge or a university newspaper. I'm thinking something in the range of 800-1200 words that leads with the concrete irony of the Anthropic/OpenAI sequence and builds toward the policy argument.

AUDIENCE: General public, especially people who follow tech news but haven't looked closely at the details of this dispute. The kind of reader who saw the headlines about Anthropic vs. the Pentagon but doesn't know that OpenAI got the same safeguards days later.

CITATION STYLE FOR THE REPORT: Informal in-text attribution typical of op-eds, weaving source names and context into the prose rather than using formal parenthetical citations.

WORK PLAN: I plan to write an op-ed that walks readers through the timeline of the dispute, highlights the contradiction in how Anthropic and OpenAI were treated, and argues that the real problem is the absence of federal legislation on military AI. I may include one supporting visual, either a side-by-side comparison of Anthropic's and OpenAI's contract terms or a timeline of the key events.
