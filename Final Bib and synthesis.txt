Amodei, D. (2026, February 26). Statement from Dario Amodei on our discussions with the Department of War. Anthropic. https://www.anthropic.com/news/statement-department-of-war
This is a public statement from Anthropic's CEO released the day before the Pentagon's deadline. Amodei lays out the two things Anthropic will not budge on: using Claude for mass domestic surveillance, and deploying it in fully autonomous weapons. His argument on surveillance is that current law hasn't caught up with what AI can do, specifically its ability to take scattered commercial data like location history and browsing records and assemble it into a detailed picture of someone's life at massive scale. On autonomous weapons, he says the technology just isn't reliable enough yet. He also points out a contradiction in the government's threats: one calls Anthropic a security risk, the other says Claude is essential to national security. This is one of the most important sources here because it's Anthropic's own reasoning, unfiltered.
Bethe, R., & Iyer, K. (2026, February 24). Pentagon threatens to make Anthropic a pariah if it refuses to drop AI guardrails. CNN. https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/24/tech/hegseth-anthropic-ai-military-amodei
This CNN report covers the February 24 meeting between Defense Secretary Hegseth and Amodei where the ultimatum was delivered. It reports that the Pentagon wanted Anthropic to allow Claude to be used for "all lawful use" and that Anthropic's two sticking points were AI-controlled weapons and mass surveillance of American citizens. The article also explains that the supply chain risk label is normally reserved for companies tied to foreign adversaries like Russia or China, which makes its use against an American company pretty unprecedented. It provides useful context on how the negotiations had been going on for months before things went public.
Rozenshtein, A. Z. (2026, February 25). What the Defense Production Act can and can't do to Anthropic. Lawfare. https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/what-the-defense-production-act-can-and-cant-do-to-anthropic
Rozenshtein, a law professor at the University of Minnesota and former DOJ attorney, digs into whether the Defense Production Act actually gives the government the power to force Anthropic to strip its safety features. He makes a key distinction: removing the contractual restrictions on how Claude can be used is legally ambiguous, but forcing Anthropic to retrain the model itself would basically be demanding a product the company doesn't make, which the DPA wasn't designed for. He also brings up the Supreme Court's major questions doctrine as a potential obstacle. His biggest takeaway is that this whole fight exists because Congress never passed laws governing military AI, so the question of whether the Pentagon can use Claude for surveillance comes down to whoever happens to be running the DOD at the time. Really strong legal analysis that's relevant for the solutions section.

They also claim they keep control over their safety stack and have OpenAI personnel embedded with the military. Interestingly, OpenAI says it asked the Pentagon to offer these same terms to all AI labs, including Anthropic. This source matters because it raises the obvious question: if the Pentagon accepted these safeguards from OpenAI, why did it punish Anthropic for asking for basically the same thing?

The Anthropic-Pentagon Dispute: AI Safety, Government Coercion, and the Case for Federal Legislation
Kevin Lu
Advanced Writing
Northeastern University
Introduction
      In late February 2026, the U.S. Department of Defense and Anthropic, the AI company behind the chatbot Claude, had a very public falling out. The core issue was simple: Anthropic did not want Claude used for mass domestic surveillance or in fully autonomous weapons, and the Pentagon wanted the right to use it for "all lawful purposes" with no restrictions. When Anthropic refused to back down, the government canceled its $200 million contract, designated the company a "supply chain risk" (a label normally reserved for foreign adversaries), and ordered every federal agency to stop using Anthropic's products (Bethe & Iyer, 2026). Then, just hours later, OpenAI announced it had signed its own classified-network deal with the Pentagon that included essentially the same prohibitions Anthropic was punished for demanding (OpenAI, 2026).
      The situation has since escalated into active litigation, with Anthropic filing two federal lawsuits alleging that the government retaliated against the company for its speech on AI safety (Allyn, 2026). The dispute touches on a lot of things at once: civil liberties, the legal limits of executive power, the role of AI in warfare, and whether a private company can set ethical conditions on technology it sells to the military. This report pulls together what's been reported and analyzed so far, looking at the legal and policy landscape around the dispute and the structural solutions that different experts have proposed.
Current Landscape
      The confrontation moved fast once it went public. On February 24, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth called Amodei to the Pentagon and told him that if Anthropic didn't comply by Friday at 5:01 PM, the consequences would include not just losing the contract but potentially being forced to cooperate under the Defense Production Act (Bethe & Iyer, 2026). In a statement on February 26, Amodei laid out Anthropic's reasoning. On surveillance, he argued that current law hasn't caught up with AI's capabilities. Under existing rules, the government can already buy Americans' location data, browsing history, and financial records from data brokers without a warrant. AI makes it possible to take all of that scattered information and automatically assemble it into a detailed profile of any individual at massive scale (Amodei, 2026). On weapons, his argument was more technical: frontier AI models are just not reliable enough yet to make life-or-death targeting decisions without a human in the loop.
      The legal situation is messier than either side would probably like to admit. Rozenshtein (2026) provides what is probably the most thorough legal analysis of the DPA threat, writing for the legal publication Lawfare. He points out that the DPA is a Korean War-era statute that was built for physical goods like steel and aluminum, not disputes about software safety features. He draws a useful line between two different things the government might demand: dropping the contractual usage restrictions on Claude (legally contested but arguably within the DPA's reach) versus forcing Anthropic to actually retrain the model to remove its safety behavior (which starts to look like demanding a new product the company doesn't make). Rozenshtein also notes that the Biden administration previously used the DPA to make AI companies report on training activities and red-teaming results, which cuts both ways: it means the DPA clearly applies to AI, but it doesn't mean every demand under it is legal. His conclusion is that the whole conflict exists because Congress has never passed legislation setting rules for how the military can use AI, so these questions get fought out through contract negotiations and threats instead.
      That structural problem, the absence of any federal rules, is also central to Ekbia's analysis (Munoz, 2026). Ekbia, who directs the Academic Alliance for AI Policy at Syracuse, argues that Anthropic's situation is essentially inevitable given how the AI industry works. Most public AI funding in the U.S. comes from defense, so companies either compromise their stated values or lose access to a huge revenue stream. He calls the competitive environment a "moral economy of the jungle" where the companies willing to be the most aggressive tend to win. His conclusion is blunt: society cannot rely on the AI industry to self-regulate, no matter how good the intentions are. The case of Anthropic, a company that literally built its brand around safety, proves that even the most committed firms can be forced into an impossible position if there are no laws backing them up.
      One factor that complicated the Pentagon's strategy was the AI workforce. More than 300 Google employees and over 60 OpenAI employees signed an open letter titled "We Will Not Be Divided," which called on their companies to hold the same red lines as Anthropic (Silberling, 2026). The letter's argument was that the Pentagon was deliberately trying to split the industry by making each company afraid the others would cave. Google DeepMind Chief Scientist Jeff Dean posted on X that mass surveillance violates the Fourth Amendment, and Altman said publicly that he didn't think the DPA should be used against AI companies. But Ekbia (Munoz, 2026) raises a good point about how durable this workforce pressure actually is. In a tighter job market, employees may not feel as safe speaking out, which means this kind of solidarity could be hard to sustain.
      The OpenAI deal is where things get really complicated. Hours after Anthropic was blacklisted, OpenAI posted a blog entry saying its new Pentagon contract includes prohibitions on mass domestic surveillance, autonomous weapons, and social credit-type systems (OpenAI, 2026). OpenAI also said it asked the Pentagon to offer the same terms to all AI labs and to try to resolve things with Anthropic. Altman later admitted the deal was rushed and acknowledged it looked "opportunistic and sloppy." The contract language actually went through revisions after legal experts found that the initial wording left loopholes around commercially acquired data. The central question this raises is obvious: if the Pentagon accepted these safeguards from OpenAI, then the dispute with Anthropic was never really about the safeguards themselves. It was about who gets to set conditions.
      Since then, Anthropic has taken the fight to court. On March 9, the company filed two federal lawsuits: one in San Francisco federal court and another in the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals (Allyn, 2026). The suits argue that the supply chain risk designation was retaliation for Anthropic's protected speech about AI safety, not a legitimate national security determination. Major tech industry groups filed amicus briefs making the point that if the government can blacklist a company over a policy disagreement, the entire procurement system starts to depend on political favor instead of the rule of law (Fried, 2026). A court hearing on whether to grant Anthropic temporary relief is set for March 24.
      It is also worth putting this dispute in an international context. The question of whether AI should be allowed to autonomously select and kill targets is not something only Anthropic cares about. In December 2024, the UN General Assembly voted 166-3 in favor of a resolution supporting a framework to prohibit or regulate lethal autonomous weapons systems (Perrin, 2025). The UN Secretary-General and the International Committee of the Red Cross have called for a binding treaty on autonomous weapons by the end of 2026. The U.S. has resisted a preemptive ban, but the overwhelming international support shows that Anthropic's insistence on human-in-the-loop requirements aligns with a broad global consensus, not some niche concern.
Solutions and Next Steps
      The fix that keeps coming up across the research is federal legislation, and it is hard to argue against. Rozenshtein (2026) puts it most directly: if Congress had set clear rules on autonomous weapons and surveillance, Anthropic would probably have been fine selling to the military, and none of this would have happened. Right now, the question of whether the Pentagon uses AI for mass surveillance depends entirely on who is running the Department of Defense at any given moment. That changes with every administration, which means any protections that exist through contract negotiations or internal policy can be undone overnight. Congress could pass legislation requiring judicial authorization for AI-driven domestic surveillance and mandating human oversight in any AI system involved in lethal targeting decisions. These are not radical positions. The Pentagon itself publicly said it wasn't interested in either of those use cases. Writing them into law would take the pressure off individual companies and put the responsibility where it actually belongs, with elected representatives who are accountable to the public.
      Legislation alone is not enough, though, if individual companies can still be picked off one at a time. The open letter from Google and OpenAI employees showed that there is real support among engineers for shared ethical standards across the AI industry (Silberling, 2026). But as long as each company negotiates with the government on its own, the Pentagon can always play them against each other, which is exactly what happened here. An industry-wide framework, modeled on the kind of professional standards that govern medicine or law, could make it much harder for the government to single out and punish one company for holding a line that the entire industry agrees on. The challenge is that the competitive structure of the AI industry actively works against this kind of coordination. As Ekbia (Munoz, 2026) points out, companies have strong incentives to undercut each other, especially when defense contracts worth hundreds of millions are on the table. That said, the "We Will Not Be Divided" letter is evidence that at least the workforce wants to move in this direction, and that pressure from below could push companies toward collective commitments even when executives are reluctant.
      The international legal landscape adds another important dimension. The UN General Assembly voted 166-3 in favor of a framework to regulate lethal autonomous weapons, and the Secretary-General has called for a binding treaty by the end of 2026 (Perrin, 2025). If the U.S. participates in and eventually ratifies such a treaty, it would create a durable legal floor for the kind of restrictions Anthropic was advocating. That kind of international commitment would be far harder to undo than any single company's contract language, and it would signal to the defense industry that human oversight in weapons systems is not a negotiable preference but a legal requirement.
      The ongoing litigation may also produce lasting structural change. Anthropic's lawsuits argue that the supply chain risk designation was retaliation for the company's protected speech about AI safety (Allyn, 2026). If courts agree, the ruling would establish a legal precedent protecting companies that publicly advocate for responsible AI practices from government punishment. The tech industry amicus briefs make clear that the stakes go well beyond Anthropic: if the government can blacklist a company over a policy disagreement, the entire procurement system starts to depend on political alignment rather than the quality of the technology (Fried, 2026). A ruling in Anthropic's favor would not just help one company. It would change the calculus for every AI firm deciding whether to push back on government demands in the future.
Conclusion
      This dispute is bigger than one contract or one company. It is about who gets to decide the limits on how powerful AI systems are used by the government, and what happens when a company tries to draw those lines on its own. Anthropic took a massive financial hit because its leadership decided that the risks of unrestricted military AI were not acceptable. The company was then punished for that stance while a competitor got essentially the same protections by being less confrontational about it.
      The lesson is that corporate ethics alone are not going to solve this. The structural vulnerability at the center of this crisis, the fact that there are no federal rules governing military AI, means that the safety of these systems currently depends on the individual courage of whoever happens to be running an AI company at the time. That is not a sustainable model. Congress needs to legislate clear boundaries, the AI industry needs collective commitments it cannot easily abandon under competitive pressure, and the courts need to establish that the government cannot retaliate against companies for exercising their right to speak on AI safety. Until those structural changes are in place, the next version of this crisis is just a matter of time.
